📈 Total Return & CAGR

The most basic question: did the strategy make money? But raw return without context is meaningless — a 50% return over 10 years underperforms a savings account.

Total Return

(final_equity − initial) / initial × 100

Raw percentage gain over the full backtest period. Always compare against buy-and-hold for the same period to judge whether the strategy added value.

CAGR (Annualised Return)

(end/start)^(1/years) − 1

Compounded Annual Growth Rate normalises return to a yearly figure. This is the right metric for comparing strategies across different time horizons.

CAGR RangeContextRating
> 25% / yrExceptional — verify not overfitExcellent
15–25% / yrStrong outperformance vs. S&P 500 (~10%)Good
8–15% / yrCompetitive — check risk metricsAcceptable
< 8% / yrBelow index; trading costs may not be worth itPoor

⚖️ Risk-Adjusted Returns

A strategy that returns 30% by taking enormous risk is worse than one returning 20% with low volatility. Risk-adjusted metrics quantify the return per unit of risk.

Sharpe Ratio

(Rp − Rf) / σp

Return above risk-free rate per unit of total volatility. The industry standard for comparing strategies. Values above 1.0 are considered acceptable; above 2.0 is excellent. Assumes returns are normally distributed.

Calmar Ratio

CAGR / |Max Drawdown|

Return per unit of worst-case loss. Less sensitive to short-term noise than Sharpe. A Calmar above 1.0 means the strategy earned back its worst drawdown in a year.

MetricPoorAcceptableGoodExcellent
Sharpe Ratio< 0.50.5–1.01.0–2.0> 2.0
Calmar Ratio< 0.50.5–1.01.0–2.0> 2.0
⚠ Sharpe on short backtests is unreliable. A 6-month backtest with 5 trades has no statistical power. Require at least 30+ trades and 2+ years of data before trusting a Sharpe ratio.

📉 Drawdown Analysis

Drawdown measures how much the strategy lost from its peak before recovering. This is the lived experience of the investor — a strategy with a −60% drawdown will be abandoned before recovery.

Maximum Drawdown

(trough − peak) / peak × 100

The worst peak-to-trough decline in the backtest. This is a hard floor — if you can't stomach this loss psychologically, you will exit the strategy at the worst time.

Drawdown Duration

days from peak to new high

How long it takes to recover from the worst drawdown. A strategy with −20% drawdown that takes 3 years to recover is far worse than one that recovers in 3 months.

Max DrawdownInterpretationRating
0% to −10%Low risk, typical for bond-like strategiesExcellent
−10% to −25%Acceptable for equity strategiesGood
−25% to −50%High risk — most investors will capitulateRisky
> −50%Extreme — only viable with small position sizeDangerous

🎯 Win Rate & Profit Factor

Win rate alone is meaningless. A strategy that wins 90% of the time but loses 10× on each loss is a disaster. The combination of win rate and average win/loss size determines profitability.

Win Rate

winning_trades / total_trades × 100

Percentage of trades that close with a profit. Trend-following strategies often have low win rates (30–45%) but large average wins. Mean-reversion strategies often have high win rates (60–80%) but smaller wins.

Profit Factor

gross_profit / gross_loss

Total profit divided by total loss in absolute terms. A Profit Factor above 1.0 means the strategy is profitable. Above 1.5 is good; above 2.0 is excellent. Below 1.0 means losing money overall.

💡 Consistency check: Win Rate × Average Win should exceed (1 − Win Rate) × Average Loss. A 40% win rate is fine if average win = $3 and average loss = $1. Check both numbers in the trade log.

⏱️ Exposure & Trade Count

How much time is the strategy in the market? Low exposure means lower risk but also means the strategy misses most of the data — and statistical significance suffers.

Exposure Time

bars_in_position / total_bars × 100

Percentage of time with an open position. Very low exposure (<5%) often means the strategy rarely trades — a few lucky trades inflate the metrics. Very high exposure (>95%) suggests the strategy is always in the market.

Trade Count

number of closed trades

Statistical significance requires a minimum of ~30 trades. Fewer than 10 trades makes any metric — Sharpe, win rate, profit factor — statistically meaningless. More trades = more reliable statistics.

🚨 Fewer than 10 trades: Any Sharpe ratio or win rate computed from fewer than 10 trades is noise, not signal. Increase the backtest period or use the Strategy Playground to test on synthetic data with more bars.

🔬 Walk-Forward Validation

Walk-forward validation is the most important test for detecting overfitting. A strategy tuned on all available data will always look good in-sample — the question is whether it works on data it has never seen.

In-Sample (first 70%)

The "training" period. Strategies optimised on this data will naturally perform well here. High in-sample performance alone proves nothing.

Out-of-Sample (last 30%)

The "test" period the strategy never saw during optimisation. This is the only honest measure of real-world performance. Look for consistent (not necessarily equal) performance.

PatternDiagnosisAction
OOS Sharpe ≥ IS Sharpe × 0.7 Robust — strategy generalises well Deploy
OOS Sharpe 0.3–0.7× IS Partial decay — some overfitting Investigate
OOS Sharpe < 0.3× IS or negative Overfit — performance doesn't transfer Discard
OOS positive, IS negative Lucky OOS — likely random noise Discard

⚠️ Common Pitfalls

Most backtest results are too good to be true. Here are the most common reasons a backtest fails in live trading.

Look-Ahead Bias

Using future data to make a past decision. Example: using tomorrow's closing price to decide whether to buy today. This system validates against it with AST checks, but always inspect the strategy logic manually.

Overfitting

Tuning parameters until the strategy looks perfect on historical data. The walk-forward validation in this system is specifically designed to detect this. A strategy with 10+ parameters and 50 trades is almost certainly overfit.

Survivorship Bias

Testing only on stocks that still exist today ignores all the companies that went bankrupt or were delisted. This system uses yfinance which only provides current tickers — be aware of this limitation.

Slippage & Costs

This system uses a 0.2% commission per trade. Real trading also incurs bid-ask spread, market impact, and borrowing costs for shorts. High-frequency strategies are most affected — a strategy with 200 trades/year loses 40% of CAGR to commission alone at 0.2%.

Regime Change

A strategy tuned on the 2020–2022 bull market will struggle in a bear market. Use the Strategy Playground to stress-test across bear/sideways/volatile regimes before trusting any backtest result.

Data Snooping

Running hundreds of strategy variants and picking the best one inflates apparent performance. If you generate 50 strategies and take the best Sharpe, expect at least 3× degradation in live trading. Use walk-forward validation and limit post-hoc selection.

⚖️ Position Sizing

Position sizing is the single biggest determinant of whether a strategy survives long enough to be profitable. A perfect strategy with wrong sizing will blow up; a mediocre strategy with disciplined sizing can survive.

Fixed Fractional (Kelly-inspired)

size = f × capital / stop_distance

Risk a fixed percentage f (typically 1–2%) of total capital per trade. If capital = $10,000, f = 2%, and stop is $5 away: size = $200/$5 = 40 shares. This ensures no single loss can wipe out the account.

Volatility Targeting

size = target_vol / (ATR / price)

Scale position inversely with current volatility. In calm markets you take larger positions; in turbulent markets you automatically reduce size. This keeps daily P&L variance roughly constant across market regimes.

Full Kelly Criterion

f* = (p×b − q) / b

Where p = win probability, q = 1-p, b = win/loss ratio. Kelly maximises long-run geometric growth. In practice, use half-Kelly (f*/2) to reduce variance — full Kelly produces extreme drawdowns.

In this system

self.buy() ← uses 100% of cash

By default, backtesting.py's exclusive_orders=True with no size= argument means the strategy uses all available cash per entry. This simplifies analysis but overstates real-world returns. Add size=0.25 to limit each trade to 25% of capital.

💡 Practical rule: Never risk more than 2% of total capital on a single trade. With a $10,000 account and a 2% rule, 10 consecutive losses still leaves $8,171 — survivable. With 5% per trade, 10 losses leaves $5,987 — psychologically devastating.

🌡️ Reading Market Regimes

The same strategy can be profitable in one regime and deeply unprofitable in another. Before deploying any strategy, identify the current regime and whether the strategy is designed for it.

Bull Market Signals

Price above 200-day MA • 200-day MA sloping up • New 52-week highs outpace new lows • Advancing stocks outnumber declining stocks • VIX below 20.

Best strategies: Momentum, trend following, breakout.

Bear Market Signals

Price below 200-day MA • MA sloping down • Most stocks hitting 52-week lows • VIX above 30 • Defensive sectors (utilities, staples) outperforming.

Best strategies: Short-selling, inverse ETF trend following, cash.

Sideways / Ranging Market

Price oscillating between support/resistance • 200-day MA flat • ATR declining • Bollinger Bands squeezing. Can last months.

Best strategies: Mean reversion, Bollinger squeeze setup (wait for breakout).

High-Volatility / Crisis

VIX above 40 • Large daily gaps • Correlations spiking toward 1.0 • News-driven moves dwarf any technical signal.

Best strategies: Reduce position size sharply. Most systematic strategies fail here.

💡 Simple regime filter: Before running any strategy, check if SPY (or the stock) is above or below its 200-day SMA. Above = use trend/momentum strategies. Below = use mean-reversion or reduce exposure to 50%.

🧮 Trade Expectancy & Risk/Reward

Expectancy is the average profit/loss per dollar risked across all trades. It's the most important single number that determines whether a strategy is viable — more fundamental than win rate or Sharpe.

Expectancy Formula

E = (P_win × Avg_win) − (P_loss × Avg_loss)

Where P_win = win rate, P_loss = 1 − win rate. A positive expectancy means the strategy makes money on average per trade. This must be positive before looking at Sharpe or CAGR.

Risk/Reward Ratio

RR = Avg_win / Avg_loss

A 1:2 risk/reward means your average win is 2× your average loss. Paired with a 40% win rate: E = 0.4×2 − 0.6×1 = +0.2 per unit risked. Profitable. A 1:1 RR requires >50% win rate to break even.

Minimum Viable Combinations

Win 30% → need RR ≥ 2.33:1
Win 40% → need RR ≥ 1.5:1
Win 50% → need RR ≥ 1.0:1
Win 60% → need RR ≥ 0.67:1
Win 70% → need RR ≥ 0.43:1

Why Trend-Following Works with Low Win Rates

A typical Turtle-style strategy wins 35% of trades but has an RR of 3:1. E = 0.35×3 − 0.65×1 = +0.4 per trade. The few big winners more than pay for all the small losses. This is why cutting losses quickly and letting winners run is the cardinal rule of trend following.

🚀 From Backtest to Live Trading

The gap between backtest performance and live performance is almost always negative — and larger than expected. Understanding why helps you set realistic expectations.

Execution Slippage

Market orders fill at worse prices than the bar close used in backtests. For liquid stocks on daily bars, this is typically 0.1–0.3% per trade. For intraday strategies on small caps it can be 0.5–2%. Use limit orders where possible.

Bid-Ask Spread

Not modelled in backtests using close prices. Add an extra 0.05–0.1% per trade for large-cap stocks, 0.1–0.5% for small-caps. This system uses 0.2% commission which approximates spread + brokerage for mid-large caps.

Psychological Discipline

The hardest part. During drawdowns, the natural response is to stop following the system. The Turtle traders who followed rules mechanically outperformed those who "improved" them. Define your rules in advance and follow them exactly.

Live Performance Expectation

A realistic adjustment for a well-tested strategy: Live CAGR ≈ Backtest CAGR × 0.6–0.7. If backtest shows 20% CAGR, expect 12–14% live. If backtest shows 5%, live may be negative after costs. Use this conservative adjustment when sizing positions.
